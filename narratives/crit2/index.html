I developed high-quality activities and projects for DSCI 210: Data Science

Back to top

I taught DSCI 210 for my first time in Fall 2019 and Spring 2020. I inherited a quality curriculum outline with solid content from my colleague Dr. Todd Iverson. One area where I saw room for improvement was with in-class activities and class projects.

Activities

Building upon Dr. Iverson’s content, I developed a suite of near-daily activities to illustrate many of the concepts he had brought into the course. Many of these activities drew from data brought to the Statistical Consulting Center, which I had been directing for a couple years. Here are some examples.

I developed rubrics to evaluate presentations, visual aids, written reports, and client meetings for STAT 370: Statistical Consulting and Communication.

Back to top

As associate professor, I taught STAT 370 four times (Fall 2019, 2021, 2022, 2023). This course requires students to write frequent reports and design PowerPoints to accompany their oral presentations. There was no consistent way of evaluating these so I developed two rubrics that I felt were successful in distinguishing between various qualities of student reports and presentation slides.

As the number of clients we served in the course continued to increase, I also wanted a way to help students understand the components of a leading a quality statistical consulting meeting. Accordingly, brand new in Fall 2023, I developed a “client meeting” rubric that I used to hold students accountable for preparing and leading professional, helpful meetings with clients. Each student was evaluated with this rubric four times in Fall 2023:

30 points total Excellent (10 points) Poor (1 point)
Professionalism Student demonstrated high professionalism throughout and had a pleasant demeanor. Nonverbal cues were entirely professional. Student appeared disinterested in the client, perhaps sounding condescending and irritated. The student’s nonverbal cues were highly unprofessional.
Preparation Student was very well-prepared. Student demonstrated they had reviewed all relevant information before the meeting and had prepared questions regarding the material, if applicable. Student clearly had not reviewed the materials the client provided before the meeting. Student seemed disoriented during the meeting.
Information gathering Student asked pertinent questions and fully gathered all information needed to advise the client on next steps. The student made sure they understood the client’s research questions, data, etc. Student had a difficult time asking questions and ended the meeting before fully understanding the client’s research questions, data, etc.

I improved and revitalized the content of and assignment structure in DSCI 310: Data visualization

Back to top

I taught DSCI 310 the first three times it was ever offered at Winona State, each fall 2015-2017. I took a few years off and revisited the course in Fall 2021. I have taught the course as Associate professor three times: Fall 2021, 22, and 23. The content and structure of the course is drastically different now than it was the first three times I taught it. There are two major ways the course is improved: increased conceptual focus, and regular peer review.

Increased conceptual focus

The first three times I taught the course, the entirety of my notes were screenshots of Tableau (a visualization software). The content was essentially, “How to use Tableau.” An example from my Fall 2017 notes:

All of the software “how to” has now been pushed entirely into video tutorials for the students to watch, and in its place is a much more comprehensive set of PowerPoints on principles of data visualization and guidelines for making good graphs. These notes focus much more on helping students [design more effective graphs], and contains content I have aggregated myself across a variety of sources.

My most up-to-date notes include:

  • Grammar of graphics

  • Visual principles for comparing quantities

  • Principles for visualizing groups

  • Design considerations

    • Color

    • Clutter

    • Focusing audience attention with preattentive attributes

My first few activities in DSCI 310 are completely devoid of software, emphasizing design and principles over technology:

  • Grammar of graphics activity

  • Grouping principles activity

  • Decluttering and focusing activity

With this redesign, learning Tableau is a medium for achieving the learning objective of creating effective graphs goal rather than the goal itself, which results in a much more satisfactory and quality class.

Regular peer review

Learning how to be an effective critic of your own work is key to improving as a data visualizer. I can critique a student’s work until I’m blue in the face, but to really take the next step a student needs to be able to look discerningly at their own work. How can I, as an instructor, help my students be better critics? To this end, beginning in Fall 2022, I implemented a peer review system for every major design task I assigned students:

  • Each student is required to submit a first draft of their assignment to CrowdGrader, a website that facilitates anonymous peer review.

  • Each student is assigned a small number (5-6) of their classmates’ first drafts. Students evaluate each draft using a rubric I provide, and are also required to provide specific, actionable feedback on the draft.

  • After receiving their peer feedback, students are allowed to incorporate it into a final draft they submit to me for a final grade.

Below is an example of the peer review process.

Not only is the feedback received by each student valuable in creating an improved final draft, but the student who submits the first draft must also provide feedback for 5-6 peer visualizations, thus being exposed to a variety of different visual approaches to the same data set while sharpening their critical eye.

For each design assignment, I evaluate not only the quality of the final drafts, but the quality of feedback each student provides their peers during the feedback process. This ensures students provide high-quality feedback and are practicing being specific in ways to improve each visualization they review.

The students’ feedback and comments on course evaluations suggest that students generally bought into the peer review process as a helpful way to improve as a data visualizer.

Students have rated the new-and-improved DSCI 310 class very favorably. Out of 58 students responding to course evaluations across 3 semesters, 51/58 (88%) of students have rated the overall quality of the course as “Excellent” or “Good.”

I proposed and developed a brand new course – STAT 380: Advanced statistical modeling

Back to top

Beginning Fall 2021, our STAT subgroup identified a gap in our curriculum. Not covered deeply in any of our courses are generalized linear models, an important family of models used for modeling non-quantitative responses, as well as methods for correlated data. I spearheaded a proposal for a new course- STAT 380: Advanced Statistical Modeling that filled this curricular gap and developed curriculum for and taught this course in its first-ever offering Spring 2023.

  • Colab notebooks of course notes

  • Colab notebooks of in-class activities

In course evaluations, 13/16 students rated the class as “Excellent” or “Good;” 16/16 agreed or strongly agreed that the course was challenging; 13/16 agreed or strongly agreed that the course colab notebooks provided them with what they needed to succeed in the course; and 15/16 agreed or strongly agreed that the in-class activities helped them understand the course material.

We will continue offering this class every other year to provide our STAT and DSCI majors with a unique elective covering an important subset of methodology previously lacking in our curriculum.

It is very important that I create a classroom environment where students can trust me, where they learn from me, where they respect me, and where they know that they can seek out help from me at any time and that I want them to learn. To make sure I am succeeding in creating this environment, on end-of-term course evaluations I ask all students whether they strongly agree, agree, disagree, or strongly disagree with the following statements:

  1. The instructor presents the material in a clear manner.

  2. The instructor exhibits a genuine enthusiasm and enjoys teaching the course.

  3. The instructor’s expectations of the student are clear.

  4. The instructor is willing/able to provide help outside of class.

  5. The instructor has a thorough knowledge and understanding of the subject matter.
