I was very grateful to receive a sabbatical during the academic year 2020-21. I enjoyed the break from teaching and the chance to engage in some very interesting applied research with excellent collaborators from around the country. My primary sabbatical research project was a major collaboration with Dr. Tricia Miller, Dr. Adam Duerr, and Missy Braham of Conservation Science Global; and Dr. Todd Katzner and Manuela Huso of U.S. Geological Survey. Over the last several years my collaborators had collected a large data set containing millions of GPS locations from 79 Bald Eagles, 30 free-flying birds captured in winter, 5 from rehabilitation facilities, and 44 tagged as nestlings. The resulting data are measured on very short intervals, as little as every 5 seconds, resulting in millions of geospatial and biological measurements. The below photos shows the same 7-week old bald eagle from above, after the telemetry device was attached and a close-up of the device.

The richness and extent of the data collected by these devices call for an automated process to classify and interpret the animal movement data. A defining feature of many previous methods developed for interpreting animal movement is that they were initially illustrated with telemetry data collected at relatively long time intervals. My role in this research was to apply statistical methods to that could be used to automate classifying risk to eagles from wind turbines using short-interval GPS telemetry data. To support my research efforts during sabbatical, Dr. Miller and I submitted a grant proposal to the American Eagle Foundation for $19,950.27. This was primarily to make up some of the income lost due to taking a sabbatical for an entire year. The grant was fully awarded.

The first part of my sabbatical research involved applying a framework for using K-means clustering to classify bird behavior using points from short time interval GPS tracks. My efforts on this part of the research resulted in an article on which I am first-author in Ecology and Evolution.

  • Bergen, S., Huso, M. M., Duerr, A. E., Braham, M. A., Katzner, T. E., Schmuecker, S., & Miller, T. A. (2022). Classifying behavior from short-interval biologging data: An example with GPS tracking of birds. Ecology and Evolution, 12, e08395. https://doi.org/10.1002/ece3.8395

The next step in my work involved classifying behavior using environmental features. The behaviors we were interested in classifying corresponded to different levels of risk from wind turbines. To carry out the classification I used boosted classification trees (BCTs) as implemented in the open source XGBoost package. XGBoost is a “big data” analysis tool that is computationally feasible even with billions of data observations, affording users a scalable and efficient implementation of BCTs with dramatic implications for model training. In this part of my research I applied BCT modeling with XGBoost to predict three categories of risk of bald eagle colliding with wind turbines in Iowa, and compared its predictive performance and computational time to other supervised learning methods.

I am the lead author on a second article summarizing this work in the highly-touted Methods in Ecology and Evolution.

  • Bergen, S., Huso, M. M., Duerr, A. E., Braham, M. A., Schmuecker, S., Miller, T. A., & Katzner, T. E. (2023). A review of supervised learning methods for classifying animal behavioural states from environmental features. Methods in Ecology and Evolution, 14, 189–202. https://doi.org/10.1111/2041-210X.14019

In addition the manuscripts, we submitted an abstract to the 2020 Wind Wildlife Research Meeting (that was held virtually Dec 1-4, 2020), which was accepted. This presentation centered mostly on the first part of my research, using K-means to cluster bald eagle flight.
