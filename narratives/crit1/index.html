Criterion 1: Teaching Effectiveness

Dec 19, 2023

During my time as associate professor I taught the following courses:

  • FIN 335 – Forecasting methods

  • DSCI 210 – Data science

  • DSCI 310 – Data visualization

  • STAT 110 – Fundamentals of Statistics

  • STAT 303 – Introduction to Engineering Statistics

  • STAT 360 – Regression analysis

  • STAT 370 – Statistical consulting and communication

  • STAT 380 – Advanced statistical modeling

  • STAT 405 – Biostatistics

  • STAT 450 – Mathematical Statistics I

  • STAT 460 – Mathematical Statistics II

The following are ways in which I demonstrated Teaching Effectiveness during the past five years:

  • I developed high-quality activities and projects for DSCI 210: Data Science;

  • I developed rubrics to evaluate presentations, visual aids, written reports, and client meetings for STAT 370: Statistical Consulting and Communication;

  • I developed a successful and high-quality online learning experience for STAT 301: Statistical Thinking for Healthcare;

  • I improved and revitalized the content of and assignment structure in DSCI 310: Data visualization;

  • I created a suite of Colab notebooks, activities, and assignments for FIN 335: Forecasting;

  • I proposed and developed a brand new course – STAT 380: Advanced statistical modeling;

  • I created a suite of Colab notebooks, activities, and assignments for STAT 360: Regression analysis;

  • I continued a record of being an enthusiastic, helpful, and clear instructor in all my courses.

I developed high-quality activities and projects for DSCI 210: Data Science

I taught DSCI 210 for my first time in Fall 2019 and Spring 2020. I inherited a quality curriculum outline with solid content from my colleague Dr. Todd Iverson. One area where I saw room for improvement was with in-class activities and class projects.

Activities

Building upon Dr. Iverson’s content, I developed a suite of near-daily activities to illustrate many of the concepts he had brought into the course. Many of these activities drew from data brought to the Statistical Consulting Center, which I had been directing for a couple years. Here are some examples.

Activity Example 1: WSU vehicle requests

The screenshot below shows data on requests for WSU vehicles:

Some of the tasks students had to do:

  • Clean “Destination City,” which was very messy as requesting parties could just type in their destination resulting in many variants of the same place (e.g. St. Paul; St paul; st paul; ST Paul St Paul/MPLS).

  • Pull off number of passengers, which is embedded in the Vehicle(s) field.

  • Compute average trip length for each vehicle, which requires differencing Departure from Return.

  • Find number of vehicles used for each trip, which required splitting the Vehicle(s) column when it contained multiple vehicles.

Activity Example 2: trout spawning data

The data below came from a consulting client, measuring redds (spawning nests) of stream trout.

From a data science perspective, there are many issues with this data that students had to grapple with including:

  • Highlighting indicating grouping status;

  • Different sections of streams contained in different spreadsheets;

  • Bold lines indicating 1000-yard segments;

  • One separate Excel file for each of 4 different years.

Activity Example 3: rape myth agreement

This data set also came from a consulting client working on a counsellor’s education master’s thesis. She wanted to compare rape myth belief as measured by the Illinois Rape Myth Acceptance Scale before and after a training on gender-based violence among student athletes. Students needed to recode all of the Likert-scale items to come up with a single score for each student, then match the scores on the pre- and post-training data to develop the clean data set on the right.

Project example 2: MUDAC 2020

This final project build on MUDAC 2020, which focused on understanding whether various motions made during civil rights trials could predict the outcome of the trial. Some of the questions students had to answer with this data involved whether filing rates differed by district, and how the prevalence and frequency of motions impacted likelihood of cases being settled or taken to trial. Students had to use association rules, a recently learned technique, to analyze the former.

I developed rubrics to evaluate presentations, visual aids, written reports, and client meetings for STAT 370: Statistical Consulting and Communication.

As associate professor, I taught STAT 370 four times (Fall 2019, 2021, 2022, 2023). This course requires students to write frequent reports and design PowerPoints to accompany their oral presentations. There was no consistent way of evaluating these so I developed two rubrics that I felt were successful in distinguishing between various qualities of student reports and presentation slides.

As the number of clients we served in the course continued to increase, I also wanted a way to help students understand the components of a leading a quality statistical consulting meeting. Accordingly, brand new in Fall 2023, I developed a “client meeting” rubric that I used to hold students accountable for preparing and leading professional, helpful meetings with clients. Each student was evaluated with this rubric four times in Fall 2023:

30 points total Excellent (10 points) Poor (1 point)
Professionalism Student demonstrated high professionalism throughout and had a pleasant demeanor. Nonverbal cues were entirely professional. Student appeared disinterested in the client, perhaps sounding condescending and irritated. The student’s nonverbal cues were highly unprofessional.
Preparation Student was very well-prepared. Student demonstrated they had reviewed all relevant information before the meeting and had prepared questions regarding the material, if applicable. Student clearly had not reviewed the materials the client provided before the meeting. Student seemed disoriented during the meeting.
Information gathering Student asked pertinent questions and fully gathered all information needed to advise the client on next steps. The student made sure they understood the client’s research questions, data, etc. Student had a difficult time asking questions and ended the meeting before fully understanding the client’s research questions, data, etc.
